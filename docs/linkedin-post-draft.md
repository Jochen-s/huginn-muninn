What if the best response to disinformation isn't fact-checking?

We've been approaching disinformation backwards. Traditional fact-checking tells people they're wrong -- and decades of psychology research shows that triggers pushback, not persuasion. But a remarkable 2024 study in Science (Costello, Pennycook & Rand) found something different: AI-driven Socratic dialogue -- where you acknowledge someone's concern first, then ask questions instead of declaring truth -- produces a 20% durable reduction in conspiracy beliefs. That's the largest measured effect of any single intervention ever published.

I've been building an open-source tool based on this research. Huginn & Muninn doesn't just check facts -- it finds common ground. Every analysis answers three questions: What is true? How are you being played? What do we actually share? It uses a 6-agent AI pipeline running entirely on local, open-source language models. Your data never leaves your machine.

The insight that changed my thinking: people on opposite sides of polarized issues overestimate how extreme the other side is by a factor of 2x (More in Common, 2019). And people who consume the most news have the most distorted view -- not the least. The problem isn't a lack of information. It's that we're being played against each other, and the tools we use to manage knowledge don't help us see it.

I'll be presenting this work at PKM Summit 2026 in Utrecht (March 20-21) -- because your second brain deserves an immune system. The project is open source and contributions are welcome.
