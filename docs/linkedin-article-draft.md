## Article (~1,500 words)

Last week at the PKM Summit in Utrecht, someone asked me a question I didn't have a clean answer for: "How do you reach someone who believes in chemtrails without making them feel stupid?"

It's a good question. And it's the question that drove me to build Huginn & Muninn, an open-source AI framework for disinformation analysis that does something no other fact-checker does: instead of just telling you what's false, it helps you find what you share with the people you disagree with.

### The Problem With Fact-Checking

We've been approaching disinformation backwards. Traditional fact-checking tells people they're wrong. Decades of psychology research shows this triggers psychological reactance: the harder you push, the harder they push back. Facebook's fact-checking labels reduce resharing by only 10-15%. And a 2025 study found that AI fact-checks can actually decrease discernment: when an AI mislabeled true headlines as false, belief dropped by 12.75%. When the AI expressed uncertainty about false headlines, belief in those headlines increased by 9.12%.

The tools we have are not working. Not because the facts are wrong, but because facts alone don't address the actual problem.

### The Actual Problem

People don't believe conspiracy theories because they're stupid. They believe them because the theories address real psychological needs that the truth doesn't. The need to make sense of a confusing world. The need to feel that someone is in control, even if that someone is malicious. The need to belong to a group that "gets it."

Research on psychosocial maturity (Loevinger's ego development model) suggests that susceptibility to conspiracy theories and authoritarian attitudes peaks at specific developmental stages, independent of intelligence or education. This isn't about being smart or dumb. It's about having the cognitive framework to handle complexity without defaulting to simple us-versus-them narratives.

And here's what makes it worse: there are organized actors who deliberately exploit this. A study in Environmental Health (Goldberg et al. 2021) documented 28 specific tactics used by industries, from tobacco to fossil fuels, to manufacture doubt about science. Five of these tactics appeared across every industry studied. The playbook is real, documented, and still in active use.

### Three Questions Instead of One

Most fact-checkers answer one question: Is this true or false? Huginn & Muninn answers three:

**1. What is true?**
Not "here's the verdict." Instead: here's the evidence for and against. Here are the actors involved. Here's what's verifiable and what isn't. You decide.

**2. Who benefits from me feeling this way?**
This is the question that changes everything. When you're angry about chemtrails, or scared about vaccines, or outraged about immigration: who profits from that emotion? Not the person sharing the article. The actors behind the narrative. Once you learn to ask this question reflexively, you become resistant to manipulation across every domain. You don't need an AI for this. You just need the habit.

**3. What do we share?**
The healing question. Not "we're all human" in the abstract. Something concrete: "You and the people you fear both worry about providing for your families. You both want clean air and safe communities. Polling shows 73% agreement on the underlying concern. The disagreement was manufactured."

These three questions work as a system. The first grounds you in evidence. The second builds your manipulation literacy. The third reconnects you to the people you've been taught to fear. Each one corrects the weaknesses of the others.

### The Science (and Its Limits)

The framework draws on a 2024 study in Science (Costello, Pennycook & Rand) showing that AI-driven Socratic dialogue produces a 20% durable reduction in conspiracy beliefs. That study won the 2026 AAAS Newcomb Cleveland Prize.

But I want to be honest about the limits. A 2025 follow-up (Bao et al.) found that while AI dialogue reduces belief in specific falsehoods, it can also degrade independent discernment over time. Users start relying on the AI instead of thinking for themselves. 21% of participants became what the researchers called "Dependency Developers."

This finding changed our entire design philosophy. Huginn & Muninn is not an oracle that gives you answers. It's a cognitive gymnasium that teaches you a mental framework. The three questions are the exercise. The goal is to make the tool unnecessary.

We built this into the architecture: forced self-assessment before the AI shows its analysis. Progressive withdrawal of AI support as your skills grow. Rate-limited access so you can't outsource your thinking. Independence checkpoints. A graduation model. Research from Wharton shows that system-regulated AI access produces double the learning gains compared to unrestricted access. Structure builds independence; unlimited access builds dependency.

### Back to Chemtrails

So how do you reach someone who believes in chemtrails?

You don't start with "that's false." You start with the three questions.

**What is true?** Condensation trails are a well-understood atmospheric phenomenon. Weather modification programs (cloud seeding) do exist and are publicly documented. This kernel of truth is what the conspiracy narrative exploits.

**Who benefits from this belief?** Content creators who monetize fear. Political actors who benefit from institutional distrust. The chemtrail narrative uses at least four of the 28 documented manufactured doubt tactics.

**What do we share with believers?** The concern is real: worry about environmental contamination, distrust of institutions that have historically lied (tobacco, lead paint, asbestos), a desire to protect family health. The question isn't whether to care. It's whether the chemtrail explanation actually helps you protect your family, or whether it redirects your energy away from documented, actionable environmental threats.

You close not with a declaration, but with a question: "If the government were spraying chemicals, what evidence would convince you it was happening? And what evidence would convince you it wasn't? Is there a difference in your standards of proof for each direction?"

That question, asked with genuine curiosity rather than condescension, is worth more than a thousand fact-checks.

### What's Next

Huginn & Muninn is open source and runs entirely on your own hardware. Your data never leaves your machine. We're building this in collaboration with academic researchers studying the relationship between psychosocial development and susceptibility to anti-democratic attitudes, with the goal of publishing peer-reviewed research on whether stage-calibrated Socratic dialogue can build lasting discernment skills rather than just changing specific beliefs.

The code is a start. The research is a start. But the real work is cultural: learning to see the person behind the position, the shared concern beneath the manufactured divide, and the manipulation that profits from keeping us apart.

The three questions are free. You don't need our tool to use them. Start today.
