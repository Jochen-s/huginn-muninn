Scientific Consensus / Mainstream Explanation

The established scientific or institutional explanation for this phenomenon.

The established behavioral genetics and cognitive science position, as represented by the APA 1996 task force and Nisbett et al. 2012 American Psychologist review, holds that the cause of between-group IQ score differences is not genetically established and is most parsimoniously explained by environmental, historical, and socioeconomic factors. Here is the substantive evidence.

IQ scores have risen approximately 3 points per decade throughout the 20th century across every tested population (Flynn 1984, 1987 Psychological Bulletin). This means the average person in 1900 would score roughly 30 points lower on today's tests -- a staggering magnitude that cannot be explained by genetic change, which operates on timescales of thousands of years, not decades. The Flynn Effect is the single most powerful evidence that the IQ phenotype is highly environmentally plastic. If IQ were primarily genetically fixed, this generational rise would be impossible.

Dickens and Flynn (2006, Psychological Science) documented that the Black-white IQ gap in the United States narrowed by approximately 4-7 points between 1970 and 2000. This narrowing is too rapid for genetic causation. Genetic composition of populations does not change measurably in 30 years. The narrowing directly implicates environmental improvements -- reduced lead exposure, improved school quality, reduced poverty rates, reduced explicit discrimination -- as causal factors.

IQ gains of 30+ points per century are incompatible with genetic explanation; genes cannot change that fast

The Black-white gap has narrowed measurably in a single generation, ruling out primarily genetic causation

Every population shows Flynn gains when environmental conditions improve, regardless of ancestry

The transracial adoption studies provide the closest thing to a natural experiment on this question. Scarr and Weinberg (1976) found that Black and mixed-race children adopted into white middle-class families showed IQ scores substantially above the Black average and approaching or reaching the white average. Moore (1986) found that Black children adopted by white families scored 13 points higher than Black children adopted by Black working-class families. These studies directly demonstrate environmental plasticity.

The Weinberg, Scarr, and Waldman (1992) follow-up to the Minnesota Transracial Adoption Study is the hereditarian side's strongest counter-evidence and deserves direct engagement: Black adoptees' scores regressed toward the Black population mean by adolescence, which hereditarians cite as evidence that early environmental gains do not persist and that genetic factors reassert themselves over time. However, several methodological limits substantially weaken this interpretation. First, the follow-up had high attrition: only approximately 55% of the original sample was located and tested, introducing selection bias. Second, the sample was small, reducing statistical power and increasing the influence of individual variation. Third, and most importantly, the follow-up was conducted during adolescence, a period in which racial identity development in a predominantly white adoptive environment introduces confounds not present in early childhood: adolescent Black children raised in white families often face heightened racial awareness, social friction, and identity challenges that are known to affect academic engagement and test performance. These are environmental pressures specific to adolescent racial identity development, not evidence of genetic reassertion. The early childhood results, collected before these identity pressures emerge, remain the more methodologically robust data point and are more consistent with environmental influence. The 1992 follow-up is important evidence that should be acknowledged, but its methodological limits prevent it from overturning the early childhood findings.

Steele and Aronson (1995, Journal of Personality and Social Psychology) demonstrated that merely reminding Black students of their racial identity before a cognitive test significantly reduced their performance, while removing the racial prime eliminated the gap. This effect has been replicated in multiple populations and testing contexts. It demonstrates that IQ test performance is sensitive to social context, motivation, and identity threat in ways that affect the measured gap without touching genetic factors.

Awareness of negative stereotypes about one's group creates cognitive load and performance anxiety. This is not about believing the stereotype -- it operates even in people who explicitly reject it. The interference is automatic and measurable in physiological stress responses.

Stereotype threat research has produced practical interventions (value affirmation, growth mindset framing, identity-safe environments) that measurably reduce achievement gaps in controlled studies, providing further evidence that environmental and psychological factors are causally active.

Epigenetic research shows that environmental exposures -- stress, nutrition, toxin exposure, trauma -- alter gene expression in ways that affect cognitive development and can be heritable across generations without changing DNA sequence. Adverse childhood experiences, poverty-related stress, and historical trauma can produce measurable effects on cognitive outcomes through epigenetic pathways. This is a relatively new field, but it provides a biological mechanism by which historical and social conditions could produce heritable cognitive differences without those differences being 'genetic' in the sense hereditarian researchers mean.

Lead exposure, which was disproportionately concentrated in lower-income and minority communities through leaded paint and gasoline, is estimated to have reduced IQ scores by 2-5 points in exposed populations. Reyes (2007, NBER) documented that gasoline lead phaseout in the 1970s-80s contributed to crime rate declines and cognitive improvements that were geographically and demographically correlated with exposure patterns.

This is the central logical issue in the entire debate. Within-group heritability measures how much of the variation in IQ within a population is associated with genetic variation within that population. It does not -- and cannot -- tell us anything about the causes of differences between populations. Jensen himself acknowledged this distinction in his 1969 paper. The standard illustration: plant the same seeds in rich soil and in nutrient-poor soil. Within each plot, the height variation among plants will be highly heritable (same soil, same conditions -- differences come from genetics). But the difference in average height between plots is entirely due to the environment (different soil). High within-group heritability is fully compatible with the between-group difference being entirely environmental.

H^2 (between-group) cannot be inferred from h^2 (within-group). This is not contested. It is textbook behavioral genetics. The hereditarian argument that high within-group heritability implies genetic between-group differences commits a logical error that population geneticists recognize and that Jensen acknowledged before committing it anyway.

Turkheimer, Haley, Waldron, D'Onofrio, and Gottesman (2003, Psychological Science) demonstrated a further complication that directly undermines the use of high heritability estimates from advantaged samples: heritability of IQ varies dramatically with socioeconomic status. In impoverished environments, the heritability of IQ is near zero -- essentially all variation is explained by environmental differences. In affluent environments, heritability approaches 0.7-0.8. This means that the high heritability estimates derived from white, middle-class, Western twin samples cannot be generalized to populations living under different environmental distributions. Groups that experience higher rates of poverty, environmental deprivation, and chronic stress will show lower heritability and higher environmental contribution to IQ variance, regardless of their genetic endowment. This finding further undermines the between-group genetic inference: the high within-group heritability figures cited by hereditarians are drawn precisely from the advantaged samples where heritability is highest, and cannot be assumed to apply to groups whose members are more likely to occupy the low-SES range where heritability is suppressed by environmental variation.

The American Psychological Association convened a broadly constituted task force in 1996 specifically to provide an authoritative scientific summary after the Bell Curve controversy. The report 'Intelligence: Knowns and Unknowns' (Neisser et al., American Psychologist 1996) is the most credible single institutional statement on the question. Its conclusion regarding between-group differences: 'The cause of that differential is not known.' The report affirmed high within-group heritability, the reality of score gaps, and the predictive validity of IQ tests, while explicitly refusing to endorse genetic causation of group differences. This is not political correctness -- it is a distinguished panel declining to assert what the evidence does not support.

Nisbett, Aronson, Blair, Dickens, Flynn, Halpern, and Turkheimer's comprehensive review in American Psychologist (2012) is the most authoritative recent synthesis. Key findings: (1) the Black-white gap has narrowed substantially since the 1970s, consistent with environmental causation; (2) environmental interventions (early childhood education, reduced lead exposure, poverty reduction) produce measurable IQ gains; (3) adoption studies show large environmental effects on group-level scores; (4) genetic factors that explain within-group differences have not been shown to explain between-group differences; (5) the available evidence is most consistent with environmental causation. The review explicitly names the within-group / between-group heritability distinction as the key logical issue.

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have identified polygenic scores for IQ using European population samples. When these scores are applied to African-ancestry populations, their predictive validity drops substantially. European-derived polygenic scores show substantially reduced predictive validity in African-ancestry populations, most consistent with population-specific LD structure and environmental confounding rather than shared causal genetic architecture, though this does not in itself definitively rule out all genetic contributions to group differences. The cross-ancestry transfer failure is not the pattern expected if the same genetic variants were driving IQ variation in both populations at comparable effect sizes, and it is most parsimoniously explained by population stratification and LD structure differences rather than shared causal architecture.

Inferential Gap

Where the evidence ends and the leap begins.

There are documented average IQ test score differences between self-identified racial groups in the United States. IQ tests have meaningful predictive validity for educational outcomes. Within-group heritability of IQ in adults is substantial. These three facts are real and are not in serious scientific dispute.

The leap is from 'within-group heritability is high' to 'between-group differences are primarily genetic'. This leap is a known logical fallacy in population genetics. It is the plant-in-different-soil error: within each plot, height is heritable; the difference between plots is environmental. Jensen acknowledged this gap in 1969 and crossed it anyway using an analogy rather than evidence. Every subsequent hereditarian paper has repeated the crossing without improving the evidence. The APA 1996 task force, Nisbett et al. 2012, and the broader genetics consensus identify this exact inference as the point where the claim breaks down.

Cofnas's burden-of-proof argument deserves direct engagement here. He argues that without a specific identified environmental mechanism explaining the entire gap, the genetic hypothesis should be the default. This is a substantive philosophical claim about where the null hypothesis lies. However, the standard scientific burden of proof places the positive causal claim as the one requiring positive evidence: to assert that specific genetic variants cause between-group IQ differences, one must identify those variants, demonstrate cross-ancestry PGS transfer, or establish candidate gene mechanisms. None of these have been produced. The environmental hypothesis, by contrast, already has identified mechanisms with empirical support: the Flynn Effect demonstrates massive environmental plasticity of the IQ phenotype; adoption studies show large score gains from environmental improvement; stereotype threat research identifies a specific, replicable psychological mechanism; lead exposure disparities are documented and quantified; and school funding, nutrition, and wealth gaps are measured and correlated with outcome gaps. The environmental hypothesis is not a residual 'we don't know, so maybe environment'; it has a positive evidential case. The genetic hypothesis, by contrast, remains a conjecture without identified causal variants. The burden of proof rests where it belongs: on the party asserting specific genetic causation.

The secondary leap is from 'some people criticize this research' to 'this research is being suppressed'. Jensen, Rushton, Lynn, Gottfredson, and Cofnas all published continuously in academic venues, held tenured positions, sold millions of books with major publishers, and appeared on major media platforms. Criticism, peer review rejection of methodologically inadequate papers, and social consequences for public statements are features of normal scientific functioning, not suppression. The 'suppression' framing transforms standard academic scrutiny into evidence of persecution, which conveniently makes the claim unfalsifiable: any rebuttal becomes further evidence of suppression.

The inferential gap is not accidental. It does specific rhetorical work: it allows the claim to appear grounded in uncontested facts (score gaps are real) while the contested part (genetic causation) is smuggled in as an implication. A reader who accepts the facts and does not know the within/between heritability distinction walks away believing the genetic conclusion is supported by the factual foundation. The smuggling is the technique.

Common Ground

Opposing positions share more concrete agreement than the public debate suggests. The disagreement is concentrated at the causal inference level, not at the level of basic observations.

IQ test score gaps between self-identified racial groups are real and documented

Both the APA 1996 task force and hereditarian researchers agree that measured score gaps exist in US data. This is not in dispute. The disagreement is about what these gaps measure and what causes them.

IQ tests have meaningful predictive validity for educational and occupational outcomes

The APA 1996 report, Nisbett et al. 2012, and the hereditarian literature all accept that IQ scores predict performance outcomes better than chance. Both sides use IQ data as meaningful. The disagreement is about what IQ measures and whether gaps in scores reflect gaps in innate capacity.

Ideological pressures can distort scientific research

This is agreed on both sides, though the examples differ. Environmental researchers cite Pioneer Fund ideological funding of Jensen, Rushton, and Lynn over six decades as a documented case of ideology distorting science (Tucker 2002, University of Illinois Press). Hereditarian researchers cite the Lysenko affair and post-2010 replication crisis in social psychology. Both concerns are legitimate. The irony is that the strongest documented case of ideological funding distorting race science is the Pioneer Fund itself, not mainstream academia.

The educational achievement gap represents a genuine and serious social problem requiring explanation

Both sides agree the gap exists and that understanding its causes matters enormously for policy. The disagreement is about which causes are primary. Nisbett et al. 2012 and the hereditarian literature both treat this as a serious empirical question. The dispute is about evidence, not whether the question matters.

Within-group heritability of IQ is substantial (estimates range 0.50-0.80 in adults)

This is accepted in both the APA 1996 report and hereditarian literature. The APA task force affirmed high within-group heritability. The critical disagreement is that within-group heritability cannot be extrapolated to between-group differences -- a point Jensen himself acknowledged in 1969 before making the inference anyway.

The actual scientific disagreement is narrow and precise: whether between-group IQ score differences are primarily caused by genetic factors or primarily by environmental, historical, and socioeconomic factors. This is an empirical question that has not been resolved by the available evidence. The APA 1996 task force concluded: 'The cause of that differential is not known.' That conclusion -- honest acknowledgment of genuine uncertainty -- is where the evidence currently sits.
