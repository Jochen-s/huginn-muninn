## How Do MI Principles Apply to Conversations About False Beliefs?

**Key finding: MI's core principles map well to conspiracy conversations, though the application is novel and underexplored in formal research.**

Motivational Interviewing (MI), developed by William Miller and Stephen Rollnick, is built on the principle that people are more likely to change when they articulate their own reasons for change rather than being told why they should change. Originally developed for addiction counseling, its principles have been successfully applied to vaccine hesitancy, health behavior change, and -- more recently -- to belief revision conversations.

### Core MI Principles Applied to Disinformation Conversations

1. **Express empathy**: Understand the person's reasons for believing the conspiracy without judgment. This is not agreement; it is comprehension. "I can see why that explanation feels right, given what you've experienced."

2. **Develop discrepancy**: Help the person notice the gap between their stated values (e.g., wanting truth, protecting family) and the implications of the conspiracy belief. Do not point out the gap; help them discover it.

3. **Roll with resistance**: When a person pushes back, do not push harder. Resistance is information about what they value. Redirect rather than confront.

4. **Support self-efficacy**: Affirm the person's ability to evaluate evidence and reach their own conclusions. "You clearly care about getting this right."

**Connection to H&M**: The Bridge Builder's Socratic dialogue already embodies principles 1 and 2 implicitly. Making the MI mapping explicit could strengthen the dialogue protocol, particularly principles 3 (rolling with resistance) and 4 (supporting self-efficacy).

## OARS Technique Adapted for Conspiracy Conversations

OARS (Open questions, Affirmations, Reflections, Summaries) is MI's primary interaction skill set. Here is how each maps to disinformation conversations:

### Open Questions

Standard MI: "What concerns you about your drinking?"
Adapted for conspiracy: "What first made you think this might be true?"

The goal is to understand the person's evidence landscape and emotional motivations without leading them. Open questions reveal the reasoning chain, which is essential for identifying where the chain breaks.

**Effective open questions for conspiracy conversations:**
- "What would it take for you to change your mind about this?"
- "How did you first come across this idea?"
- "What would the world look like if this were true? And if it weren't?"
- "What's the strongest piece of evidence you've seen for this?"
- "If a friend came to you with this claim, how would you help them evaluate it?"

**Questions to avoid:**
- "Don't you think that's unlikely?" (closed, leading)
- "Where did you hear that?" (implies the source is suspect)
- "Have you considered that you might be wrong?" (triggers identity defense)

### Affirmations

Standard MI: "It takes courage to talk about this."
Adapted: "I can tell you've spent a lot of time thinking about this."

Affirmations acknowledge effort, critical thinking, and values -- not the conclusion. Research shows that affirm is the only MI behavior that both increases change talk AND decreases sustain talk (PMC4936274).

**Effective affirmations:**
- "You clearly care about truth, otherwise you wouldn't have looked into this so deeply."
- "It takes intellectual honesty to question the official story."
- "Your skepticism of powerful institutions comes from a real place."

**Affirmations to avoid:**
- Anything that validates the specific conspiracy content
- Generic praise ("that's interesting") that sounds dismissive
- Affirmation of the conspiracy's emotional narrative ("you're right to be angry")

### Reflections

Standard MI: "It sounds like you're frustrated with how things are going."
Adapted: "It sounds like you feel the official explanation doesn't account for everything you've observed."

Reflections serve two purposes in conspiracy conversations:
1. **Simple reflections** demonstrate listening and build trust
2. **Complex reflections** can gently amplify a contradiction the person has expressed

A powerful MI technique is the **overstated reflection**, where the practitioner reflects the belief back slightly more extremely than stated. This can cause the person to back away from their own position. Example: Person says "I think the government is hiding something about the vaccine." Overstated reflection: "So you believe the entire medical establishment is deliberately harming people." The person often self-corrects: "Well, not deliberately harming, but..."

### Summaries

Summaries in MI serve as checkpoints. In conspiracy conversations, they allow the practitioner to organize what has been said and -- crucially -- to juxtapose contradictory elements the person has expressed:

"Let me make sure I understand. You believe [specific claim], and the evidence you find most convincing is [their evidence]. At the same time, you mentioned that [contradictory element they stated]. Does that capture it?"

This is not a gotcha. It is genuine comprehension-checking that creates space for the person to notice their own inconsistencies.

## How Does Costello et al. (2024) Map to MI?

The Costello, Pennycook & Rand study (Science, 2024) achieved a 20% durable reduction in conspiracy beliefs using GPT-4 Turbo in personalized evidence-based dialogues with 2,190 participants. The approach was described as "factual, evidence-based" rather than MI-based, but the mapping is illuminating.

### What Costello et al. did that aligns with MI:

1. **Personalization**: Participants described their own conspiracy belief and evidence in their own words. The AI used THIS specific material, not generic counter-arguments. This mirrors MI's emphasis on working with the client's own language and motivations.

2. **Evidence-matching**: The AI matched its counter-evidence specifically to the participant's stated reasons, not to the conspiracy theory in general. This is analogous to MI's "developing discrepancy" -- meeting the person where they are.

3. **Non-judgmental engagement**: The AI engaged with the specific conspiracy theory without dismissing the person. "Evidence matters much more than we thought it did -- so long as it is actually related to people's beliefs" (Pennycook, quoted in MIT Sloan).

### What Costello et al. did that diverges from MI:

1. **Directive persuasion**: GPT-4 was instructed to "persuade users that their beliefs were untrue." This is the opposite of MI's non-directive stance. MI would never instruct the practitioner to persuade.

2. **No emphasis on autonomy**: The AI did not include autonomy-supportive framing ("but of course, the decision is yours"). It argued directly.

3. **No rolling with resistance**: When participants pushed back, the AI continued to present counter-evidence rather than stepping back.

### Synthesis for H&M:

The Costello approach works (20% durable reduction is remarkable) but creates the dependency risk that Bao et al. (2025) documented. MI principles could address this:

- Use Costello-style personalized evidence-matching (effective)
- But frame it through MI: "Here is evidence that some people find compelling when they look at this question. What do you make of it?" (autonomy-preserving)
- Roll with resistance rather than escalating counter-arguments
- The goal is not to persuade but to create conditions for self-persuasion

**This is the Bridge Builder's exact design philosophy, but formalized through MI vocabulary.**

## Phrasings That Reduce Defensiveness

### Autonomy-Supportive Language

The single most effective defensiveness reducer is **acknowledging the person's freedom to decide**. Research on psychological reactance shows that adding "but of course, it's up to you" (or equivalent) dramatically reduces resistance. This works because reactance is triggered by perceived threats to autonomy, not by the content of the message.

**Effective patterns:**

| Pattern | Example | Why it works |
|---------|---------|--------------|
| Curiosity framing | "I'm curious about..." | Positions speaker as fellow explorer, not authority |
| Hedged suggestions | "Some people find it useful to..." | Non-directive; person can adopt or reject |
| Permission-seeking | "Would it be okay if I shared something I noticed?" | Respects autonomy explicitly |
| Shared uncertainty | "I'm not sure about this either, but..." | Levels the power dynamic |
| Values-first | "Since you care about [their value]..." | Connects to their motivation, not yours |
| Perspective invitation | "What if we looked at it from..." | Collaborative reframing |
| Autonomy reminder | "You're the one who gets to decide what to believe." | Reduces reactance directly |

### Street Epistemology Techniques

Anthony Magnabosco's Street Epistemology (SE) offers field-tested phrasings for non-confrontational belief exploration:

- **"What do you mean by [term]?"** - Clarification before disagreement. Many disagreements dissolve when definitions are clarified.
- **"If this belief weren't true, how would you discover that?"** - Forces consideration of falsifiability without asserting falsity.
- **"On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you?"** - Quantifying confidence creates room for nuance (few people say 10).
- **"What's the main reason you're at [their number] rather than lower?"** - Focuses on their strongest evidence, respecting their reasoning.
- **"A [different-tradition believer] told me they feel the same conviction about their belief. How could we tell which is correct?"** - The Outsider Test (Loftus). Creates cognitive dissonance without attacking.

## Phrasings That INCREASE Defensiveness (Avoid These)

### Reactance Triggers

Research identifies specific language patterns that trigger psychological reactance and should be avoided:

| Pattern | Example | Why it backfires |
|---------|---------|------------------|
| Controlling language | "You must understand that..." | Threatens autonomy; triggers oppositional response |
| Impossibility claims | "It is impossible to deny that..." | Frames disagreement as irrational |
| Identity labels | "People who believe this are..." | Attacks the person, not the belief |
| Patronizing corrections | "Actually, the truth is..." | Positions speaker as authority; triggers status threat |
| Ridicule | "That's been debunked" (dismissive tone) | Signals contempt; closes dialogue |
| Forced engagement | Unsolicited corrections | Creates reactance even when correct |
| Emotional appeals to harm | "Do you realize how dangerous that is?" | Triggers guilt, which produces defensive hostility |
| Direct negation | "That's not true" / "That's wrong" | Creates binary win/lose framing |

### The Backfire Effect: Current Status

The "backfire effect" (where corrections strengthen the original belief) was prominently reported by Nyhan & Reifler (2010) but has been difficult to replicate. Current consensus: **backfire effects are not robust** and may be rare in practice. However, corrections that trigger reactance still fail to produce change -- they just fail silently (no effect) rather than producing active backfire.

The practical implication is the same: **controlling, directive, identity-threatening language does not work**, even if it does not technically make things worse.

## Change Talk vs. Sustain Talk in Belief Revision

### Core Concept

In MI, **change talk** is any client statement that argues for change (in this context: openness to revising a belief). **Sustain talk** is any statement that argues for maintaining the status quo (holding the belief). The balance between these two predicts outcomes: more change talk relative to sustain talk correlates with actual change.

### Mapping to Belief Revision

| MI Concept | Belief Revision Equivalent |
|------------|---------------------------|
| Change talk | "I've wondered about that..." / "Maybe there's another explanation..." / "I haven't looked at that evidence..." |
| Sustain talk | "But everyone knows..." / "You can't trust the mainstream..." / "That's what they want you to think..." |
| Preparatory change talk | "I used to believe something similar and changed my mind before..." |
| Mobilizing change talk | "I should probably look into this more carefully..." |

### What Elicits Change Talk

Research (PMC4936274) found that specific therapist behaviors predict whether change talk or sustain talk follows:

- **Affirm**: The only behavior that both increased change talk AND decreased sustain talk
- **Open questions**: Increased change talk (but did not reduce sustain talk)
- **Simple reflections**: Increased change talk
- **Complex reflections**: Increased change talk

Confrontation, closed questions, and directive statements all increased sustain talk.

### Deep Canvassing Connection

David McRaney's work on deep canvassing (documented in "How Minds Change") describes the same dynamic: when canvassers ask people to articulate their own experiences and uncertainties, the person generates change talk spontaneously. The canvasser's role is to create space for this, not to argue.

Key deep canvassing principles that map to MI:
- Ask the person to rate their confidence (creates space for nuance)
- Ask for the personal experience behind the belief (moves from abstract to concrete)
- Share a relevant personal story (modeling vulnerability, not arguing)
- Ask "what would it take to change your mind?" (directly soliciting change talk)

## Practical Implications for Huginn & Muninn

### Bridge Builder Dialogue Protocol Enhancements

1. **Formalize OARS mapping**: The Bridge Builder's Socratic dialogue should explicitly implement the OARS sequence: begin with an open question about what convinced the user, affirm their engagement with the topic, reflect their reasoning back (including contradictions they expressed), and summarize before presenting evidence.

2. **Add autonomy reminders**: Every Bridge Builder output should include at least one autonomy-supportive phrase. "You're the best judge of what to do with this information" or equivalent. This is cheap to add and directly reduces reactance.

3. **Implement the overstated reflection technique**: When a user expresses a belief, the Bridge Builder could reflect it back slightly amplified, creating natural self-correction opportunities. This is the most powerful MI technique for belief revision and maps naturally to dialogue.

4. **Track change talk signals**: The system could detect change-talk language ("I've wondered...", "Maybe...", "I haven't looked at...") and sustain-talk language ("They always...", "You can't trust...") to calibrate response intensity. More sustain talk should trigger more open questions and fewer evidence presentations.

5. **Use Street Epistemology calibration questions**: "On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you about this?" provides immediate calibration data and creates psychological space for nuance. This could be the first question in every Bridge Builder dialogue.

### What H&M Should NOT Do

- Never tell users they are wrong, misled, or manipulated
- Never use controlling language ("you must consider", "the evidence clearly shows")
- Never present corrections as unsolicited (always frame as response to user's question)
- Never use emotional appeals to harm ("do you realize the damage this causes?")
- Never label the user's belief process negatively ("that's a logical fallacy")
- Instead: name the technique being used on them, not the error they are making
